What is Sickle Cell Disease?

Sickle Cell Disease, also known as Sickle Cell Anemia, is a genetic disorder in the red blood cell that affects its ability to transport oxygen around the body. This disorder also changes the shape of some red blood cells as many of the cells would be shaped like a "sickle".

Location

Humans normally have Hemoglobin A molecules, but those with Sickle Cell Disease have defective Hemoglobin S molecules. Sickle Cell Disease is caused by a mutation in the Hemoglobin Beta Gene, which is located in Chromosome 11. This causes those affected to have Hemoglobin S molecules instead. These mutated blood cells then impact organs like the heart.
Prevelance
Currently, Sickle Cell Disease is more common in developing nations spread in the continents of Asia and Africa. In the world, there are approximately more than 1,000,000 people that have the sickle cell genetic trait. Although the USA isn't a developing nation, Sickle Cell Disease is the most common genetic disease in the country (2021, NCBI). In Sub-Saharan Africa, 45% of the population currently carry the Sickle Cell Genetic. This impacts 3% of births. In India, around 20 million people carry this genetic.This contrasts to the continent of Europe as they only have approximately 52,000 cases of this disorder.
