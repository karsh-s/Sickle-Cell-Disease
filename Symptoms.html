Symptoms
Anemia
Those with Sickle Cell Disease have weaker crescent shape cells. This crescent shape is a disadvantage in many ways. It firstly has a smaller surface area to volume ratio compared to normal red blood cell, which disallows it to carry large masses of oxygen around the body. Secondly, the shape makes the blood cell weaker. This means the cell survives for less time than the normal red blood cell. These scenarios both cause there to be a lack of oxygen around the body. In the end, you may feel dizzy, light headed, fatigued and short of breath for extended periods of time.
Muscle Pain
Due to the abnormal shape of the Sickle Cell, the cells are unable to flow smoothly through the blood vessels. The blood flow is slowed down, and sometimes fully blocked. As a result, blood/oxygen is unable to reach the muscles. If you have Sickle Cell Disease, there is a chance that you would only experience these pains a couple times a year, but some experience it on a regular basis. These pains can vary in length too. You may only feel such pain for a couple of hours, but those that are unlucky would experience them for weeks (Mayoclinic, 2021).
Swelling
If you have Sickle Cell Disease, you may experience swellings around the body. This is due to large fluid build-up. This occurs commonly for Sickle Cell patients because the unusual cell shape may block the blood flow to certain tissues. This means the fluid is trapped; thus causing swellings.
Delayed Growth
Children and adolescence require nutrition, oxygen and energy to grow physically. Sickle Cell Disease slightly prevents growth, and in some cases, it delays puberty. This lack of growth is a result of the Sickle Cell's inability to efficiently transport oxygen and nutrients due to its mutation in the hemoglobin beta gene. If you are a child with Sickle Cell Disease, your growth may be slightly behind yours peers.
Prone to Infections
As many blood vessels are blocked by the Sickle Cells, organs such as the spleen are heavily impacted. The spleen is the organ that helps defend your body from viruses. By reducing the spleen's capabilities, those with Sickle Cell Disease may be more vulnerable to infections. If you have Sickle Cell Disease, you would need to be more weary than others about catching deadly infections.
Blurry Vision
Similar to the situation with the spleen, Sickle Cells block the blood vessels connected directly to the eye. In turn, the retina loses some of its capabilities as the person's vision may become blurry. If some cases, the lack of blood flow into the eye cause blindness.
